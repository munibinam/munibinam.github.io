**Abstracts of PhD Essays**
======

The Impact of Extreme Weather Events on Agricultural Productivity in the United States
---

  This essay explores the impact of extreme weather events on agricultural productivity in the United States, specifically examining the accuracy of weather data measurement. It highlights how current methods of measuring extremes might smooth out extreme values, potentially leading to biased estimates. This sequence can smooth out data extremes, possibly underrepresenting the severity of weather events and introducing biases into the research results. To address this, the study proposes refining measurement techniques to more accurately capture the effects of extreme weather events on the agriculture sector in the United States.

The Impact of Extreme Weather Events on Drinking Water Quality in the United States
---

  The second essay aims to evaluate how extreme weather events affect drinking water quality in the United States. It discusses how extreme weather events, increasing in frequency and intensity in recent years, extensively damage water infrastructure, including supply systems, treatment facilities, and distribution networks. Given the aging public water infrastructure in the United States was built according to now-outdated weather patterns, deviations from these patterns are likely affecting water quality. The study uses water quality violations under the Safe Drinking Water Act as a proxy for water quality to assess impacts of extreme weather. The goal is to understand the relationship between extreme weather and water safety and to support the development of strategies to strengthen water utilities against future extreme events.
   
Weather Extremes and Residential Water Demand: Heterogenous Effects of Housing Lot Size Composition on Residential Water Consumption – Evidence from Water Agencies in California
---

  The third essay explores the impact of recent housing developments in California on water consumption, particularly during extreme weather events like droughts. It uses a unique dataset of monthly water usage and lot sizes across different water agencies to estimate a water demand equation and assess how lot size affects residential water use. The study also examines the effectiveness of conservation policies, such as the 2015 drought mandate, in mitigating the water use impact of new housing. The findings provide policy insights on water use dynamics, emphasizing the need for region-specific water conservation strategies to address increasing drought severity and water scarcity in California.
